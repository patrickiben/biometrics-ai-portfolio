How to find hours for out‑of‑scope work hours only · no $

A do‑it playbook for the moment an unplanned deliverable lands (an ad‑hoc interim, an extra TLF pack, a sponsor re‑run) and you have to answer: can we absorb this, or is it a change order? The Excel workbook does the arithmetic; this page tells you where every number comes from and what to do with the answer.

0The core idea, in one line

Budget left is not what's left to spend. Planned − Actual looks like a cushion, but most of it is already committed to finishing work in flight. What you can actually redeploy is:

NET FINDABLE = (Σ Planned + Contingency) − Σ EAC

— the total budget plus reserve, minus the honest forecast cost of finishing everything already in scope (EAC = Estimate at Completion). Only that difference is real, spendable slack. The whole workbook exists to compute this one number honestly and tell you whether the new ask fits inside it.

1Get the planned hours

Planned hours live in the project budget / SOW. CRO budgets are built bottom‑up as a task grid: each deliverable carries budgeted hours, often split by role. For biometrics that's the SAP, each ADaM domain, each TLF batch, DSMB/interim outputs, define.xml, reviewer's guide, and so on.

1
Get the current budget grid from the PM / project finance (usually an Excel by task and role).
2
List each biometrics task as a row in the workbook's Effort Ledger and enter its Planned hours.
3
Enter the contingency / management reserve as its own row — the hours held back for the unexpected. If your budget doesn't name one, put 0 and know your only levers are slack, deferral, and a change order.

2Get the actual hours

Actuals come from the time‑tracking system — hours logged to the project code, by task, to a cutoff date.

  • Pull hours-to-date per task and enter them in Actual.
  • Correct for timesheet lag. Timesheets trail reality by a week or two. If someone has worked hours they haven't entered, add their verbal estimate — otherwise every task looks artificially under budget and your slack is a mirage.
  • Note the cutoff date you reconciled to, so the picture is defensible later.

3Forecast each task to completion (EAC)

Actual‑to‑date tells you nothing about a task that's half done. You need EAC — the total hours the task will take once finished. Pick the method that fits how much you know:

MethodEAC =Use when
Actual + ETCActual‑to‑date + your honest estimate‑to‑complete of the remaining workDefault. You can look at what's left and size it.
% completeActual ÷ (% complete)Effort tracks linearly with progress; a task 60% done at 90 h forecasts ≈150 h.
Bottoms‑upActual + Σ(re‑estimate of each remaining item)The remaining work is itemizable (e.g., 6 TLFs left × ~4 h).
CompletedActual (task is done)Task finished — EAC is just what it cost. This is your realized variance.

Enter % complete and let the workbook estimate EAC, or type EAC directly if you did a bottoms‑up. Variance = Planned − EAC: positive = forecasting under (possible slack), negative = forecasting over (a problem to raise, not a source of hours).

4Find the real slack — not the apparent slack

Not every under‑forecast is money in the bank. Grade it:

RealizedCompleted tasks that came in under Planned. Safe to redeploy — it's banked.
ProjectedIn‑progress tasks forecasting under. Trust it only if enough is done to know — a task 10% complete hasn't earned its slack yet. Discount aggressively.
IllusoryAn early task with low actual simply because it hasn't ramped. This is not slack; counting it is the #1 way find‑hours math goes wrong.

Before you offer slack to a PM, be able to say which task it comes from and why the under‑run is real. "We have 19 hours: 12 realized on ADaM QC which finished under, 7 projected on TLF QC which is 80% done and tracking light" is defensible. "The budget shows 40 hours left" is not.

5Compute net findable

With Planned, EAC, and contingency entered, the ledger computes:

NET FINDABLE = (Σ Planned + Contingency) − Σ EAC

That's your honest cushion: everything budgeted, minus the forecast to finish everything already promised. In the workbook's demo it's 59 hours. This is the number you test the new ask against.

6The find‑hours decision tree

A new ask needs N hours. Work the levers in order — cheapest/most‑within‑your‑control first:

1
Reallocate slack. Move realized/projected under‑run hours to the new task. This is inside your project; you decide it and record it (which task gave, which received).
2
Draw contingency. Spend the reserve. That's what it's for, but it's a PM decision to record — and once spent it's not there for the next surprise.
3
Defer / re‑sequence. Push lower‑priority in‑scope work later to free people now. This trades schedule for hours and needs the PM/sponsor to agree the new dates.
4
Change order. If a shortfall remains after 1–3, it is added scope. Raise a CO for the remainder. Don't quietly absorb it.

The workbook's Find Hours sheet automates the test: enter the need, and it computes

Shortfall = MAX(0, Need − NET FINDABLE)

and returns a verdict: absorbable (Shortfall = 0) or change order for X hours (Shortfall > 0).

7Have the conversation

Reconciliation is only useful if it becomes a clear ask to the PM/sponsor. Two scripts:

Absorbable

"Sponsor requested an unplanned PK re‑run — I scope it at 45 hours. Reconciliation: ADaM QC finished 12 h under and TLF QC is 80% done tracking 7 h under (19 h slack), plus 40 h contingency — 59 h net findable. I propose funding it from the 19 h slack + 26 h contingency, leaving 14 h contingency. No change order needed. OK to proceed?"

Not absorbable

"The ask scopes at 90 hours; net findable is 59. I can cover 59 from slack + contingency, but the remaining 31 hours is out of scope — here's the change‑order draft. I'd recommend we not start the out‑of‑scope portion until it's signed."

8Raise a change order

A change order is the record that scope grew and the budget was amended to match. The workbook's Change Order sheet drafts one. It states:

  • What the added work is, and why it's out of scope (not in the current SOW).
  • The hours required, by task/role.
  • The schedule impact (dates that move).
  • Approvals: lead biostatistician, PM, sponsor.

Sequence matters: raise → lead biostat + PM review → sponsor approves and signs → budget amended → then do the out‑of‑scope work. Doing it first on a handshake is how CROs eat unbillable hours.

  • A 45‑hour ask → Shortfall = MAX(0, 45 − 59) = 0 → absorbable. Fund from 19 h realized/projected slack + 26 h contingency.

Note TLF programming is forecasting 8 h over — that's a real over‑run to flag, and it's already netted into the 59. Ignoring over‑runs is how "found" hours evaporate.

Pitfalls

  • Optimistic EAC. Under‑forecasting to make the budget look healthy inflates net findable and sets you up to blow the real number. Forecast honestly, over‑runs included.
  • Treating Planned − Actual as available. Most of that gap is committed to in‑flight work. Use EAC, not Actual.
  • Counting early‑task under‑runs as slack. Low actual on a task that hasn't ramped is not a cushion.
  • Absorbing silently. If you fund out‑of‑scope work with no record, you can't defend the budget when it runs short later — and you've set a precedent that the work is free.
  • Forgetting deferral has a cost. Freeing hours by pushing work trades schedule risk for effort; say so.
  • Working before the CO is signed. Out‑of‑scope hours spent on a handshake are hours you may never get back.
