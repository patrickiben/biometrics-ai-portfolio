Finding Hours for Out-of-Scope Work

A playbook for the recurring reality: a sponsor asks for an unplanned analysis, an amendment lands, an ad-hoc table is "needed by Friday" — and it wasn't budgeted. Where do the hours come from, and when is it genuinely a change in scope?

Operational effort in HOURS only — no dollars, no rates, no headcount claims. This is how the team stewards its own effort budget.

Open the find-hours worksheet →

1The problem, stated honestly

An effort budget is a finite pool of hours allocated across deliverables. New work does not add hours to the pool; it must be funded from somewhere. The failure mode is silent absorption — quietly doing the extra work by over-running existing tasks, which shows up later as a blown budget, a missed date, or a burned-out programmer. The fix is a quick, repeatable reconciliation that makes the source of the hours explicit and recorded.

The lead biostatistician's job here is to protect the team's hours: to know where the slack is, to spend contingency deliberately, and to say "this is additional scope" when it is — so the people doing the work aren't the ones quietly paying for it.

2The reconciliation

Track three numbers per deliverable — planned, actual-to-date, and EAC (estimate at completion: what it will really take). The forecast variance tells you where the give is:

variance = planned − EAC (positive = under-running = slack; negative = over-running = at risk)
net findable = (Σ planned + contingency) − Σ EAC

Net findable is the honest headroom: the reserve plus the net of every under-run and over-run across the portfolio. Over-runs are netted in on purpose — you cannot count a task's under-run as free while another task is quietly eating the difference.

3The decision tree — where the hours come from

  1. safest Reallocate under-run slack. Move budget from deliverables forecasting under plan (EAC < planned). It is already yours; no one needs to grant it. Confirm the under-run is real (the task is genuinely tracking light, not just early).
  2. deliberate Draw the contingency buffer. The management reserve exists for exactly this. Drawing it is a decision to record, not a silent move — note how much and why, so the remaining buffer is known.
  3. negotiated Defer / re-sequence lower-priority in-scope work. If slack + contingency fall short, free hours by moving non-critical-path effort later — with the PM's agreement on the new dates. This trades time for hours; it is not free.
  4. formal Escalate a change order. If a shortfall remains after 1–3, the work is genuinely additional scope. Raise a change order for the shortfall hours. This is the correct outcome, not a failure — it keeps the budget honest and the team's hours protected.

4When it is genuinely out-of-scope

Not everything can (or should) be absorbed. Escalate to a change order when:

  • the ask exceeds net findable hours (the worksheet shows the shortfall);
  • absorbing it would push a critical-path deliverable past its date;
  • it recurs — a "one-off" that is now every month is a scope pattern, not a favor;
  • it changes the deliverable itself (a new domain, an added analysis population, an unplanned interim).
Absorbing silently is the one thing not to do. It hides the true cost, sets a precedent that the work is free, and lands on whoever is holding the task. Make the source of every hour explicit — reallocated, drawn from contingency, deferred, or change-ordered.

5Governance

  • Hours / effort only — this playbook reasons in hours, not dollars, rates, or headcount. Cost is finance's; effort stewardship is the team's.
  • Recorded, not silent — every reallocation, contingency draw, deferral, and change order is written down (the ledger and change-order template carry it).
  • Human-decided — the worksheet is decision support; the PM and lead biostatistician decide and own the call.
