IRT / RTSM User Acceptance Testing

A practical guide to UAT of the Interactive Response Technology (IRT) / Randomization & Trial Supply Management (RTSM) system before go‑live — from the biostatistics lens. The IRT is vendor‑built; the sponsor confirms it does what the spec says. The randomization is ours, so validating that the IRT implements it exactly is squarely a biostatistician's responsibility.

1What UAT for IRT/RTSM is

An IRT/RTSM system randomizes participants, assigns kits, and manages drug supply. It is configured by a vendor from the protocol and a User Requirements Specification (URS). UAT is the sponsor's confirmation, in a test environment, that the built system behaves as specified before it is used on real participants. It is a GCP / computer‑system‑validation (GAMP‑5) activity with a 21 CFR Part 11 audit trail, and it ends in a named human sign‑off.

Why it can't slip. Once the study is live, a randomization or supply defect can mis‑allocate treatment, break the blind, or mis‑dose a participant. UAT is the last gate before that risk becomes real. The sign‑off gate stays blocked until every critical test passes and every critical/major defect closes.

2The biostatistician's role

The randomization schedule is produced and owned by biostatistics. UAT is where you confirm the IRT implements your list, not a re‑derivation of it:

  • Allocation fidelity — the IRT's allocation matches the approved randomization list, participant‑for‑participant (sequence, kit, stratum).
  • Stratification — participants are allocated within the stratum they were entered under (e.g. weight, cohort).
  • Block integrity — within‑block balance matches the block size / ratio in the list; block boundaries are not leaked.
  • Allocation concealment — treatment is concealed until dosing; blinded roles see a kit ID, never the arm.
  • Dose‑escalation logic (early phase) — sentinel‑dosing holds, cohort caps, and the "next cohort blocked until SRC approval" gate behave per protocol.
  • Reproducibility — the allocation is reproducible from the frozen list; the randomization export back to biostatistics reconciles to the IRT.

4Coverage areas

The tracker groups the scripts into the areas a full early‑phase IRT UAT should cover:

  • Randomization — allocation vs schedule, stratification, blocks, concealment, uniqueness, reproducibility.
  • Dose‑escalation cohorts — sentinel dosing, cohort caps, dose‑level assignment, the SRC escalation gate.
  • Enrollment / screening — screening numbers, screen‑fail handling, re‑screening, enrollment cap.
  • Drug dispensing — kit ↔ arm, kit type by visit, visit‑window rules, dispensing‑to‑dosing reconciliation.
  • Resupply / inventory — resupply triggers, expiry, temperature‑excursion quarantine, inventory reconciliation.
  • Emergency unblinding — the workflow, role permissions, the audit trail, and safety notification.
  • Notifications / reports and data integration (EDC) — alerts, and IRT↔EDC / IRT↔biostat reconciliation.

5The go‑live sign‑off gate

A deterministic, pre‑specified gate — the study does not go live until all three are true:

  1. every critical test is Pass;
  2. zero open critical or major defects;
  3. 100% requirement traceability (no URS requirement without a test).

The tracker evaluates the gate live and shows exactly what is blocking it. The gate packages the evidence; the sign‑off is a named human decision (the tester/biostatistician), never the tool's.

6Governance

  • Synthetic test data only — UAT runs on test participants in a test environment; no PHI, no real participant data.
  • Part 11 / ALCOA++ — test execution and defects are attributable, contemporaneous, and audit‑trailed; the frozen URS and test‑script set are version‑controlled.
  • Human‑signed — UAT is approved by a named tester and the accountable biostatistician; automation packages and checks, it does not approve.
