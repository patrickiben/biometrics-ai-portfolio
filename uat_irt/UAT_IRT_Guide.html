0What a UAT is, and who does it

An IRT/RTSM (a.k.a. IXRS) is the system that randomizes participants and manages drug supply. A vendor builds it from your User Requirements Specification (URS). UAT is your confirmation, in a test environment, that the built system does exactly what the URS says — before a single real participant is randomized. It is a GCP / computerized‑system‑validation (GAMP‑5) activity with a 21 CFR Part 11 audit trail, and it ends in a signed report filed to the eTMF.

Your part, as the biostatistician. The randomization schedule is yours. Whoever else tests screening, supply, or reports, you own the confirmation that the IRT allocates per your list: right arm, right stratum, right block behaviour, concealed until dosing. If the IRT mis‑randomizes and it reaches production, it can compromise the whole study. That is why the randomization tests are Critical and why you personally reconcile IRT allocations against the list.

WhoDoes what in the UAT
IRT vendorBuilds the system from the URS; provides the UAT (staging) environment, test accounts, and a test randomization list; fixes defects and issues new builds.
UAT leadOwns the UAT plan, scripts, execution, defect triage, and the summary report. Often the lead statistician or a data manager.
Biostatistician (you)Authors/QCs the randomization & unblinding test cases; reconciles allocations against the list; signs off the randomization portion.
QAConfirms the process was followed (plan approved, scripts executed with evidence, defects closed, sign‑off complete) and that it is inspection‑ready.

Where it runs: the vendor's UAT / staging environment — never production — using test participants, test sites, and a test randomization list. Typical duration: 2–4 weeks (dry run → formal execution → defect fix cycles → regression → sign‑off).

What this preview is, and where a real UAT actually runs

This section closes the one gap the rest of the guide assumes you already know: the interactive tracker page is a teaching mock, and the real UAT happens inside the vendor's system in your browser. Read this before you conclude that anything on the tracker page is a live result.

The tracker page (UAT_IRT_Tracker.html) is a read-only illustration. It is pre-filled with synthetic test cases and shows you what a completed UAT tracker looks like and how the go-live gate behaves (it flips to READY only when every Critical test is Pass, there are zero open Critical or Major defects, and traceability is 100%). It connects to no live system, you do not run a real UAT inside it, and nothing on it is study data. It is there to show you the finished shape you are working toward.

Where the real work lives: you operate the vendor's system in a browser and record every result in the Excel workbook. Those two things are the real tools. The tracker page is the picture, the workbook is the record.

How a real UAT connects to real systems. The IRT/RTSM is a vendor-hosted web application, so you do not install anything. UAT is performed in the vendor's UAT / staging environment (a separate instance from production), loaded with a test randomization list, never the production list. Your inputs come from documents you and the study team already own: the URS (agreed between the sponsor/biostatistics and the IRT vendor), the randomization specification and the test randomization list (produced by the independent unblinded randomization statistician), the drug-supply spec (kit types, visit-to-kit matrix, resupply thresholds, expiry), and the protocol. You confirm the built system does exactly what those documents say, before a single real participant is randomized.

The step sequence below is how you get access and actually run it.

1
Request UAT access and role-based test accounts from the vendor, routed through your project manager. You need a login for each role you must test: investigator/site, unblinded pharmacist or statistician, and blinded roles. Testing that permissions differ by role is part of the UAT, so you need more than one account.
2
Confirm the staging environment is loaded with the test list and test data, not production. Verify the build/version label the vendor gives you and record it. Everything you execute is tied to that build.
3
Log into the staging web app in your browser and operate it. Register a test participant, randomize, dispense a kit, drive inventory down to trigger resupply, and invoke emergency unblinding. You are driving the real application, just pointed at the test instance.
4
Compare what the system did against your list and spec. For randomization this is your reconciliation: open the test list next to the IRT allocation output and confirm, cell for cell, that the arm, stratum, block position, and kit number match. This is the biostatistician's part and it is not delegated.
5
Capture evidence as you go. Dated screenshots and system exports from the staging app are the evidence. A pass without a dated screenshot is not inspection-ready.
6
Record every result in the Excel workbook: Status (Pass/Fail/Blocked/Not Run), tester, date, defect ID, and traceability. The workbook does not talk to the IRT system. You transcribe results into it by hand, on purpose, so the record is attributable and stands on its own in the eTMF.
7
Log every defect in the vendor's defect-tracking tool too, so the vendor fixes the build, and mirror it on the workbook's Defect Log. Retest on the new build the vendor issues, re-run the tests the change could have touched, and record the new build/version you tested against.
Two systems, one record

Think of it as two live systems and one paper trail. The vendor's staging IRT is where you act. The vendor's defect tracker is where the fix loop lives so the build gets corrected. The Excel workbook is neither of those: it is your independent record and your sign-off, and it is what an auditor reads. Screenshots and exports are the bridge between what the system did and what your workbook says it did.

What the sign-off actually authorizes. When the workbook gate reads READY and the named humans (UAT lead and the accountable biostatistician) sign the summary, that signed package is what authorizes the vendor to promote the validated build to production for real randomization. The gate packages the evidence, a person still signs. Every number in the record comes only from the validated systems (the IRT, the test list, your reconciliation), never from an assistant that helped you draft or check the wording.

1Before you start — the inputs you need

You cannot write a single test until you have these. Gather them first:

Approved URSThe signed User Requirements Specification — the contract of what the system must do. Every test traces to a URS requirement.
ProtocolFor the randomization design (arms, ratio, stratification factors, blocks), the visit/dosing schedule, and the emergency‑unblinding rules.
Randomization spec + test listThe randomization specification and a test randomization list loaded into the UAT environment (never the production list). You reconcile allocations against this.
Drug‑supply specKit types, the visit‑to‑kit matrix, resupply strategy/thresholds, expiry, and temperature‑excursion handling.
UAT environment accessURLs and test user accounts for each role (investigator/CRA, unblinded pharmacist, unblinded statistician, blinded roles) — you must test that permissions differ by role.
Test dataTest sites, test participant IDs, and the input values you'll use (weights for strata, visit dates, etc.).

2Write the UAT plan (one page is fine)

A short plan, approved before execution, that states: the system + version under test, the environment, the scope (which URS sections), the roles/testers, the pass/fail definition and the go‑live sign‑off criteria, the defect‑severity definitions, and where evidence is stored. The plan + the scripts + results + defect log + summary are the UAT package that goes to the eTMF.

The workbook's Instructions sheet is a ready‑made plan skeleton; adapt it to your study.

3Turn the URS into test cases

Read each URS requirement and ask: what would I do to prove the system does this, and what would prove it does not do the wrong thing? Most requirements need at least one positive test (it does the right thing) and one negative test (it blocks the wrong thing), plus boundary tests where a value sits on the edge of a rule.

Worked: one requirement → three test cases

URS‑RND‑02: "The system shall allocate each participant within the stratum they were entered under (stratification factor: weight <70 kg / ≥70 kg)."

  • Positive: enter a participant at 68 kg → expect allocation from the <70 kg stratum list.
  • Boundary: enter a participant at exactly 70.0 kg → expect the ≥70 kg stratum (confirm the rule's boundary matches the protocol's — is 70.0 "≥70"?).
  • Negative / integrity: confirm a <70 kg participant is never pulled from the ≥70 kg list even when the <70 block is nearly exhausted.

Group the resulting cases by area — Randomization, dose‑escalation/cohorts (if applicable), enrollment/screening, dispensing, resupply/inventory, emergency unblinding, notifications, EDC integration. The workbook's Test Scripts sheet comes pre‑filled with ~27 of these for a parallel‑group study; add, delete, and edit to match your protocol.

4Write a test script that's actually executable

A vague expected result ("it randomizes correctly") is useless — a tester can't objectively pass or fail it. Every script needs six parts:

PreconditionThe state the system/data must be in first (e.g., "test list loaded; <70 kg block 1 has ≥1 slot free").
Test dataThe specific inputs (participant ID, site, weight, visit date).
StepsNumbered, exact actions — the role you log in as and every click/entry.
Expected resultSpecific and verifiable — a value someone can check, not a feeling.
Actual resultWhat happened (tester fills at execution).
EvidenceScreenshot / report reference, attached and dated.
Worked test script — RAND‑02, stratified allocation (2:1 active:placebo, block 6)
PreconditionTest randomization list loaded in UAT; <70 kg stratum, block 1 has an unused slot; you know (as an authorized unblinded role) the next expected list entry.
Test dataParticipant 101‑001, Site 101, screening weight 68.0 kg.
Steps1. Log in as Investigator at Site 101. 2. Register participant 101‑001; enter weight 68.0 kg. 3. Click Randomize. 4. Record the returned kit/bottle number and confirmation. 5. Log in as authorized unblinded statistician; open the allocation report for 101‑001.
Expected101‑001 is allocated to the next unused entry in the <70 kg stratum, block 1 of the test list — matching arm, sequence, and kit number. The investigator confirmation shows a kit ID only, not the arm. The allocation report row equals the list row.
How you verifyOpen the test list next to the IRT allocation report and confirm, cell for cell: stratum, block position, arm, kit number. This reconciliation is the biostatistician's job.

Do this reconciliation for a sample across every stratum, every block boundary, and both arms of the ratio — not one happy‑path participant. Block boundaries (last slot of a block, first slot of the next) and the allocation ratio (does a 2:1 block really give 4 active : 2 placebo?) are where IRT builds most often go wrong.

5Execute the tests

  1. Dry run (informal). Walk the scripts once yourself in the UAT env to catch broken test data, wrong preconditions, or an environment that isn't ready. Fix the scripts, not the results.
  2. Formal execution. Execute each script in order. For each: perform the steps, compare to Expected, and fill the workbook's yellow columns — Actual, Status (Pass/Fail/Blocked/Not Run), Tester, Date — and capture a screenshot as evidence.
  3. Blocked ≠ Fail. If you can't run a test because the environment or test data isn't ready, mark it Blocked and note why; it isn't a defect in the system.
  4. Evidence discipline. Every executed test has an attributable, dated result and a screenshot. "It passed" without evidence is not inspection‑ready.

6Log & manage defects

Any Fail becomes a defect on the workbook's Defect Log (Defect ID, Severity, Status, the test it came from, description, date). Put the Defect ID back on the failed test row. Severity drives whether it blocks go‑live:

SeverityDefinitionExample
CriticalCould compromise participant safety, break the blind, mis‑randomize, mis‑dose, or corrupt randomization/supply integrity. Blocks go‑live.Wrong stratum allocation; duplicate randomization number; expired kit dispensable; a blinded role can see the arm.
MajorSignificant functional error in a key workflow, not directly safety/integrity. Blocks go‑live until fixed.Resupply not triggering at threshold; wrong kit type dispensed for a visit; safety‑notification not sent after an unblind.
MinorCosmetic or non‑functional; does not block go‑live (fix or waive with rationale).Notification email omits the site number in the subject; a label typo.

The fix → retest → regression loop

  1. Vendor fixes the defect in a new build and tells you what changed.
  2. Retest the failed test on the new build. If it passes, set the defect to Closed.
  3. Regression: also re‑run any tests the change could have touched — a fix to the block logic means re‑running the allocation tests, not just the one that failed. Note the build version you tested against.

7Traceability — no requirement without a test

On the workbook's Traceability sheet, list every URS requirement. The "Covered" column auto‑flags any requirement with no test — a coverage gap you must close before sign‑off. A requirement no one tested is a requirement no one confirmed. This is the first thing an auditor checks.

8Sign off & file

The Execution Summary sheet computes the go‑live gate. It flips from NOT ready to READY only when all three are true:

  1. every Critical test is Pass;
  2. zero open Critical or Major defects;
  3. 100% requirement traceability.

When READY, the UAT lead and the accountable biostatistician sign the summary. File the full package — UAT plan, test scripts, executed results with evidence, defect log, and signed summary — in the eTMF. That signed package is the evidence the system was validated fit for use before go‑live.

The gate packages evidence; it does not grant approval. A green gate means the pre‑specified criteria are met. A named human still reviews and signs — the tool never approves a go‑live.

Timeline, checklist & pitfalls

A realistic 3‑week shape

Week 1Gather inputs; write/adapt the plan and scripts; build test data; dry run.
Week 2Formal execution; log defects; vendor fixes; retest cycle 1.
Week 3Regression on the fix build; close defects; traceability check; summary + sign‑off; file to eTMF.

Pre‑sign‑off checklist

  • Every URS requirement has ≥1 executed test (traceability 100%)
  • Every Critical test is Pass, with evidence attached
  • Zero open Critical/Major defects; Minor defects fixed or waived with rationale
  • Randomization allocations reconciled against the test list across strata, block boundaries, and the full ratio
  • Concealment confirmed (blinded roles cannot see the arm) and role permissions tested
  • Regression run after the last fix build; build version recorded
  • Summary signed by UAT lead + biostatistician; package filed to eTMF

Pitfalls that fail inspections

  • Testing in production or against the real randomization list — always the UAT env + a test list.
  • Only happy‑path testing — you must test that screen‑fails can't randomize, expired kits can't dispense, blinded roles can't unblind.
  • Skipping boundaries — block edges, exact stratum cut‑points, the last kit in inventory, expiry exactly today.
  • No concurrency test — two sites randomizing in the same second is a classic duplicate‑number bug.
  • Not regression‑testing after a fix — a fix can break something that previously passed.
  • Vague expected results — "works correctly" can't be objectively passed; write a checkable value.
  • No evidence — a pass without a dated screenshot is not inspection‑ready.
  • The biostatistician not personally reconciling allocations — don't delegate the one thing that's yours.
